1a. WWII (Pearl Harbor)
    Our values: date="Dec 7, 1941", decline=20.3%, daysToBottom=127,
                daysToRecover=917
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

DATE: "Dec 7, 1941"
  Verified: CONFIRMED
  Pearl Harbor attack was December 7, 1941 (a Sunday). Markets opened Dec 8.
  Sources: All historical sources confirm this date.

DECLINE: 20.3%
  Verified: CONFIRMED
  The Dow closed at 116.60 on Dec 5, 1941 (last trading day before attack)
  and bottomed at 92.92 on April 28, 1942.
  Calculation: (116.60 - 92.92) / 116.60 = 20.31%
  Multiple sources cite "-20.31%" or "almost 20%" drawdown.
  Note: The S&P 500 (then the S&P 90 Composite) tracked similarly.
  LPL Research cites -19.8%. ISABELNET cites -19.8%.
  Sources: Benzinga, iSectors, Demauriac, Begin To Invest, Dividend.com
  Verdict: CONFIRMED (20.3% matches Dow calculation exactly; LPL's 19.8%
  is an S&P-specific figure)

DAYS TO BOTTOM: 127
  Verified: QUESTIONABLE (likely ~142 calendar days)
  Bottom was April 28, 1942. Dec 7, 1941 to Apr 28, 1942 = 142 calendar days.
  127 calendar days from Dec 7 = April 13, 1942 (about 2 weeks early).
  If counting TRADING DAYS from Dec 8, 1941 to Apr 28, 1942, that would be
  approximately 95-100 trading days.
  ISABELNET data says 143 days to bottom, matching 142 calendar days.
  Our value of 127 is ~15 days short of the actual calendar day count.
  Verdict: QUESTIONABLE -- should be ~142-143, not 127. Off by ~15 days.

DAYS TO RECOVER: 917
  Verified: QUESTIONABLE
  917 days from Dec 7, 1941 = June 11, 1944.
  The Dow was at 119.40 at end of 1942 (already above 116.60 pre-Pearl Harbor
  level). So the Dow recovered to its pre-Pearl Harbor close by late 1942
  (~330 days).
  However, the pre-WWII PEAK was 151.29 (April 8, 1940), not recovered until
  Dec 29, 1944 (1118 days from Pearl Harbor).
  917 days (June 1944) falls between these two interpretations.
  This may be measuring the S&P composite's specific recovery, which could
  differ from the Dow's timing. Hard to verify without daily S&P data.
  LPL Research says recovery took 450+ days.
  Verdict: QUESTIONABLE -- 917 seems too long if recovering to Dec 7 close,
  too short if recovering to pre-war peak. Possibly S&P-specific timing.

  Verified: CONFIRMED
  The S&P 500 finished the first week of trading (Sep 17-21) down 11.6%.
  This is one of the most widely cited and consistent figures across sources.
  Pre-close (Sep 10): 1,092.54. Week-end close (Sep 21): ~965.
  Sources: CNN Money, CNBC, Money.com, BIS, iSectors, LPL, Demauriac,

  Verdict: CONFIRMED

DAYS TO BOTTOM: 10
  Verified: CONFIRMED
  Sep 11 to Sep 21 = 10 calendar days. Only 5 trading days occurred
  (Sep 17-21) since markets were closed Sep 11-14.
  Sources: Multiple confirm Sep 21 as the bottom.
  Verdict: CONFIRMED

DAYS TO RECOVER: 75
  Verified: QUESTIONABLE
  Sources consistently say recovery occurred "within one month" or
  "by the one-month anniversary" (Oct 11, 2001 = 30 calendar days).
  Bullrunim specifically cites 31 days to recovery.
  75 days from Sep 11 = November 25, 2001.
  However, the S&P 500 had a complex post-9/11 trajectory: it recovered
  briefly in October but then declined again in the broader dot-com bear
  market context. The S&P hit ~1100 in early October but may not have
  sustainably held above 1092 until later.
  75 days could represent a more sustained/permanent recovery, while
  the "1 month" figure represents the first crossing above pre-9/11 levels.
  Sources: Money.com, Bullrunim, CNBC
  Verdict: QUESTIONABLE -- 30-31 days is widely cited for initial recovery.
  75 days may represent a more conservative "sustained recovery" measure.

    Our values: date="Mar 19, 2003", decline=14.7%, daysToBottom=90,
                daysToRecover=180
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

DATE: "Mar 19, 2003"
  Verified: CONFIRMED
  The Iraq War began with "shock and awe" on March 19-20, 2003 (US time
  evening of Mar 19 / Baghdad time Mar 20).
  Sources: All sources confirm.
  Verdict: CONFIRMED

DECLINE: 14.7%
  Verified: WRONG (in context)
  The -14.71% figure is real but represents the pre-war BUILDUP decline from
  December 2002 to March 2003, NOT the decline after the invasion started.
  After the invasion began (Mar 19), the S&P only fell 5.3% over 7 trading
  days before recovering in 16 days.
  Using this as "the war's decline" is misleading because:
  - The market RALLIED once the invasion started
  - The 14.7% measures the uncertainty BEFORE the war, not the war itself
  Sources: Chase de Vere, Hennion Walsh, Demauriac
  Verdict: WRONG in context. The 14.7% is the pre-invasion buildup decline,
  not the post-invasion market reaction. Actual post-invasion decline was
  only ~5.3%.

DAYS TO BOTTOM: 90
  Verified: WRONG
  If measuring from March 19 forward, the market did NOT decline for 90 days.
  It declined for ~7 trading days then rallied strongly.
  If measuring the pre-war buildup backwards, Dec 19, 2002 is ~90 days
  before Mar 19, which matches when the buildup decline started.
  But "days to bottom" should measure from the event forward, not backward.
  The actual bottom from the invasion date was ~7 trading days (~10 calendar
  days) after March 19.
  Sources: Chase de Vere, Hennion Walsh
  Verdict: WRONG -- market bottomed ~7-10 days after invasion, not 90.

DAYS TO RECOVER: 180
  Verified: WRONG (in original context)
  If the invasion-triggered decline was 5.3% and recovered in 16 days,
  then 180 days is grossly overstated for post-invasion recovery.
  If measuring recovery from the pre-war buildup decline (starting Dec 2002),
  the market did take several months to recover to Dec 2002 levels, but
  this conflates multiple market dynamics (dot-com aftermath + Iraq War).
  Sources: Chase de Vere
  Verdict: WRONG in the context of a post-invasion recovery timeline.
